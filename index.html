Alan Turing

Born: June 23, 1912
Died: June 7, 1954;
Cause of Death: Suicide
Primarily worked for: The Government Code and Cypher Code under the United Kingdom
Education: King's College, University of Cambridge, Princeton University

Biography

Alan Turing was a revolutionary British mathematician, logician, and computer scientist. Turing introduced the concept of the Turing machine and played a pivotal role in breaking the German Enigma machine codes during World War II, which lead to the Allied victory. Turing's enduring legacy also includes his influential proposal of the Turing Test as a theoretical test for machine intelligence, making him a key figure in the history of computing and artificial intelligence. Turing would later take his own life after being persecuation for his homosexuality at the age of 41 in 1954.

Notable Contributions

  • 1932: Alan Turing and others cracked the Enigma Code, used by the Nazis
  • 1936: Alan Turing introduced the concept of the Turing machine, a theoretical model of computation that laid the foundation for the development of the first modern computers
  • 1950: Alan Turing created the Turing Test, meant to determine whether a computer can think like a human
